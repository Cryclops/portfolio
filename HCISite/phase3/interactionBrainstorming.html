Explanation of Interactions

Using the activities we came up with in our Activity Design process, we now started to think about the interaction design of the interface. To do this, we looked at the principles and guidelines of Interaction Design: affordances, feedback, mappings, and constraints.


In our information design brainstorming, we thought about how each feature would look on the screen. The buttons are going to be standard touch screen buttons that are big enough for the user to use. The sliders are going to be standard as well, and the user will use his/her finger to slide up and down or left and right depending on whether the slider is horizontal or vertical.


In our Interaction Design, we took the buttons and sliders we created, and modified them so they would have good affordances, mappings, and feedback for the user. Each button has a symbol on it that corresponds to what the button does. For example, the Change of Pitch button has a music note and two left and right arrows to tell the user that the pitch can go up and down.


Each slider is placed in the design so that is maps to what it actually does. Take the volume slider for example. Its vertical design maps directly to the fact that if the user slides upward, the volume increase and the downward for a decrease. The sliders are also all labeled with abbreviated names like "VOL" for volume.


As for feedback, a lot of the feedback comes from the actual sound. If the user increased the volume, he should hear an increase in the sound's amplitude. But, if a button opens another window like the "AUTO" button for automation, feedback would be actually seeing the window open. So each button and slider takes into account good feedback.


We also needed to take constraints seriously. Our buttons needs to be big enough for the user. We needed to make sure that our sliders don't overlap. We needed to be sure that the text in the display screen was readable. So all in all, we took the 4 main principles of Interaction Design and made sure we provided enough to obey them.

Discussion with Client

We talked to our clients after the information design process and they expressed how much they liked the set up of our design. They liked our sketches and how we decided to add the sliders and buttons using various "drawers" and displays.


We then met with our main client again after doing the interaction design process. This is where we needed our client to help the most and we got a lot of good information. He helped in creating the labels for each of the buttons so that they were easy for any DJ to understand. Our client created the pan control symbol, which can be seen in our prototype.


We also let our client play with our first prototype. Granted, our prototype is no where finished, but it gave our client a basis for what our design was actually going to look like. We asked our client, Alex, to play with each button and tell us what each did before he actually used it. He understood every one.


We then asked Alex to tell us if any button provided bad feedback. He told us that the "drawers" were a little difficult to find. He said that if a user didn't know how Android phones work, they wouldn't know how to open these "drawers." We told Alex that the users of this application would have Android experience, but to put his mind at ease, we added an icon with a mixer symbol to indicate that there was something there on the drawer handle.
