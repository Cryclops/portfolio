Interaction Design Claims


Claims for Scenario #1

  • Claim 1: The search interface.
    • + Dave was able to quickly find the Miley Cirus video due to the obvious search bar on his phone. He was able to enter a textual query, rather than scroll through elaborate menus.
    • - The search feature requires Dave to type. Since his phone does not have a physical keyboard, Dave didn't have the easiest time communicating his query to the phone.
  • Claim 2: The drawer.
    • + Dave was able to utilize the play control "drawer" nicely to separate and organize his DJ thought processes. And because Dave has had experience with his phone a little while, he knew how to work the drawer feature, by sliding his finger up, bringing up the drawer.
    • - The "drawer" isn't everywhere and is a little difficult to find if you aren't familiar with the Android cell phone. You must know that you need to slide up from the bottom of the screen.
  • Claim 3: The AutoEQ.
    • + Dave quickly pushed the button labeled "AUTO" and a window popped up that allowed Dave to equalize his music.
    • - The AutoEQ screen presented another layer of software depth. Dave couldn't access his play controls, and was punished for spending too long playing with the screen.

Claims for Scenario #2

  • Claim 1: The volume/pitch combination.
    • + Clearly labeled sliders that didn't overlap allowed the DJ to quickly create his "mash-up"
    • - The DJ's precise touch wasn't perfect and he accidentally clicked out of the pitch control slider
  • Claim 2: The volume/pan combination.
    • + The DJ pressed the labeled "PAN" button that brought up the screen with sliders. The DJ then used the sliders for his desired effect
    • - The DJ's sweaty fingers again didn't have a perfectly precise touch, and the DJ made a mistake
