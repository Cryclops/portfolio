Explanation of Ideas

The ideas we came up with were at first only inspired by the results of our Field Studies. However, we soon began to think of what our clients might want, rather than what they currently like or have requested.


The "Ability to create mash-ups" idea was a perfect example of meeting our client's standard of expectations. This was a very strong sentiment throughout the field studies. The main idea behind it seemed to be that all successful DJs play at least two songs ontop of eachother. We rejected the idea of having the ability to only play one audio track, as we thought our client would dissapprove.


The "Music Cloud" idea was inspired by our listeners' answers in our field studies. They were often not impressed with the variety in a DJ's library. We thought that it would be a nice fix for the DJ to not worry about their library, as they could easily update it when the need strikes.


"Automations" was one of our new ideas. We realized that current DJ software can be hard, or cumbersome to control. We thought that automating some common tasks would help sort out some of the headache DJs experience.


Discussion with Client

After talking to our clients in our requirement analysis phase, they each talked a lot about engaging the audience through what they do with their software. They want to be able to fade between songs, play any song an audience member requests, and have the ability to alter or change the music (changing the pitch, mashing it with another song, etc.).


We then met with our main client again after doing the activity design process. He was very intrigued with our ideas. The most intriguing idea that he seemed to really take a liking to was the music cloud. He said that having the ability to play any song at any time is huge for a DJ. Most DJs only play what is in their library, but if a DJ could have the ability to meet the audience?s wants, he or she could be that much more exciting.


The second idea that our client liked was the portability. Having a DJ interface on a cell phone would allow the DJ to be more involved with the audience. He could walk around and engage them on a more personal level. Most DJ?s stand behind their equipment, but our software would allow the DJ to become one with the audience, which is ultimately why a DJ does what he does.


Our client also mentioned that having too many features on such a small interface may be difficult to navigate. He told us to keep it as simple as possible, to include the basic volume and frequency changers as well as the ability to play two songs at once. But as for the interface itself, the simpler the better.
