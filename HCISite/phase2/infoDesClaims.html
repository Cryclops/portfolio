Information Design Claims


Claims for Scenario #1

  • Claim 1: The search interface.
    • + Dave was able to quickly find the Miley Cirus video due to the obvious search button on his phone. He was able to enter a textual query, rather than scroll through elaborate menus.
    • - The search feature requires Dave to type. Since his phone does not have a physical keyboard, Dave didn't have the easiest time communicating his query to the phone.
  • Claim 2: The drawer.
    • + Dave was able to utilize the play control "drawer" nicely to separate and organize his DJ thought processes. He didn't have to worry about accidentally changing the pitch this time when changing a song, since the "drawer" was hidden when he was picking.
    • - The "drawer" isn't everywhere. When Dave was on the AutoEQ screen, he had to press the back button before the software would allow him to get to his play controls.
  • Claim 3: The AutoEQ.
    • + Although Dave is no expert DJ, he was able to accomplish an advanced task rather easily with the AutoEQ screen. Drawing on the screen was much more intuitive for him than using a knob or slider.
    • - The AutoEQ screen presented another layer of software depth. Dave couldn't access his play controls, and was punished for spending too long playing with the screen.

Claims for Scenario #2

  • Claim 1: The volume/pitch combination.
    • + Combining the volume with the pitch in the multitouch interface led to better sounding "mash-ups" and a less embarassed, more popular DJ.
    • - Even though the mistake was less obvious, to the attentive listener, it is still as embarassing. It isn't a real fix for the problem, but rather a bandage.
  • Claim 2: The volume/pan combination.
    • + Combining the volume with the pan in the multitouch interface led to a new, unique sound that isn't common, or easily possible using a standard laptop.
    • - As the DJ get's more into it, and more sweaty, he starts to lose the control he would have with physical controls.
